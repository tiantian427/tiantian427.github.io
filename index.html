About

Research profile

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Finance at Guanghua School of Management, Peking University. My primary fields are political economy, economic history, and public economics.

My research asks how state capacity is produced, borrowed, constrained, and sometimes strategically reorganized. Across projects, I examine environmental review, insurance capital, public insurance commitments, judicial systems, and opaque risk transmission. The common question is institutional rather than sectoral: when formal rules change, who actually carries the state’s authority, and with what consequences?

Research

Working papers and work in progress

My current projects study how institutional reforms reshape screening, financing, adjudication, and risk transmission under imperfect state capacity.

01
With Prof. Li-An Zhou · Draft available

Who Screens for the State? Recentralization and Local Control in Environmental Review

This paper examines a recentralization reform where environmental review authority shifted from county to city governments. We find that instead of simply strengthening capacity, the reform reorganized local protectionism: city governments created new, selective intermediary chains that retain low-visibility compliance problems. Mayoral turnover partially unravels these patterns, suggesting that recentralization can transform fragmented localism into a coordinated but politically fragile city-level form.

02
Sole Author · Draft available · 2026 ARIA Annual Meeting

The Shadow Fiscal Financier: Insurance Capital, Regulatory Shocks, and Local Government Debt in China

Identifies insurance capital as a shadow financier for local government debt. Using a granular Bartik IV based on liability-side regulatory shocks, I show that insurer yield hunger compresses LGFV credit spreads, linking financial deregulation to implicit government guarantees.

03
with T. Hao & C. Wu · Major Revision at Journal of Risk and Insurance

Accelerating Fragility: Climate Shocks, Information Contagion, and Systemic Risk in Opaque Insurance Markets

Studies how climate shocks and information contagion amplify systemic risk in opaque insurance markets, where risk transmission is difficult for outside investors and regulators to observe in real time.

04
Reject and Resubmit at Management Science

Securing the Care Workforce: How Public Insurance Commitments Reshape Human Capital Pipelines

Exploiting China’s Long-Term Care Insurance pilot and 2016–2022 Gaokao admission data, this paper shows that public insurance commitments can raise the competitiveness of nursing admissions by reorganizing hiring structures rather than directly raising wages.
